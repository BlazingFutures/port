My favourite reads

  • Little Bets

    Little Bets: How breakthrough ideas emerge from small discoveries. By Peter Eagle Sims.

    "Little Bets" advocates a shift in mindset toward embracing experimentation and learning through small, affordable actions. Rock, Schultz, and Bezos achieved success not by analyzing ideas minutely, but by making little bets to discover and refine creative possibilities. Little bets are concrete, iterative actions that help navigate uncertainty, create new solutions, and solve open-ended problems. While the Silicon Valley mantra encourages big bets, the book emphasizes that little bets are essential for learning about problems and opportunities. The key is to flip the switch from expected gains to affordable losses, acknowledging that planning the unpredictable is an illusion. Making little bets, like Jeff Bezos and expert entrepreneurs do, allows for affordable losses that may grow into significant gains, as seen in the case of Pixar under Steve Jobs.

  • The Black Swan

    The Black Swan. By Nassim Nicholas Taleb

    My favorite idea from the book: Relying on the past as a basis for predicting the future is a common human error. We often construct narratives based on historical events, expecting the future to unfold similarly. However, unforeseen factors can significantly impact outcomes. Consider the example of a turkey, fed daily and assuming the humans will always care for it. Yet, on the eve of Thanksgiving, it heads to the slaughterhouse, a surprise to the turkey but not the butcher. This illustrates that what may seem like a "black swan" event depends on one's knowledge and expectations. To avoid being misled, it's crucial to steer clear of depending on large and potentially misleading predictions. Looking back at history, humans tend to create cohesive narratives to make sense of events, hoping for predictability. To avoid falling into the narrative fallacy, it's advisable to prioritize experimentation over storytelling, experience over history, and knowledge over theory.

  • Nudge

    Nudge: Improving Decisions about Health, Wealth, and Happiness. By Richard Thaler, Cass R. Sunstein

    Reading this book I've learned that rules of thumb are instrumental in simplifying intricate decisions. Take, for instance, the complex decision structure encountered in a paint shop, where thousands of color combinations are possible. Utilizing a linear, list-based shade card would make the process overwhelmingly exhaustive. To streamline this complex choice structure, the concept of incentives is introduced as a powerful nudge. The idea is to group colors with similar characteristics, making the decision process more straightforward. Users first select the main color group, and then narrow down to specific shades within that group, effectively simplifying the decision-making process. In designing incentives as nudges, a simple heuristic is proposed: consider who uses, who chooses, who pays, and who profits. This framework helps guide the design of incentives that influence individuals to make specific choices, emphasizing the significant role of incentives as effective nudges.
